# Clinical Tremor Evaluation Actions

This document provides a breakdown of the standard tasks used in clinical neurological exams (such as the UPDRS or the Fahn-Tolosa-Marin scale) to evaluate the characteristics, severity, and type of a tremor.

### 1. Rest
- **What it is:** The patient sits completely relaxed with their hands resting limply in their lap or supported on a table.
- **What it tests:** **Resting Tremor**. If a tremor is highly active here but disappears as soon as the person starts to move their arm, it is a very strong indicator of Parkinson's disease.

### 2. Postural Hold
- **What it is:** The patient holds their arms outstretched straight in front of them, parallel to the ground, working against gravity.
- **What it tests:** **Postural Tremor**. This tests if a tremor emerges when muscles are activated to maintain a specific static position. It is very common in Essential Tremor and physiological tremors. 

### 3. Kinetic Movement
- **What it is:** A general, simple voluntary movement, such as waving the hand or flexing/extending the wrist without aiming for a specific target.
- **What it tests:** **Kinetic Tremor**. This evaluates if the tremor occurs solely due to muscles being in active motion, rather than resting or holding a posture.

### 4. Pointing (or Reaching an object)
- **What it is:** The patient moves their finger to touch a specific target (e.g., reaching for a cup, or doing the clinical "finger-to-nose" test).
- **What it tests:** **Intention Tremor**. This specifically measures if the tremor amplitude gets violently worse the closer the hand gets to the visual target. This type of tremor is highly characteristic of damage to the cerebellum.

### 5. Picking and placing
- **What it is:** The patient grips an object (like a block or a pen), moves it through space, and precisely places it somewhere else.
- **What it tests:** **Complex Task Tremor**. This requires simultaneous coordination of multiple muscle groups (shoulder, elbow, wrist) while sustaining a grip force. Tremors can behave differently when the hand is applying isometric force (gripping) versus open-handed movement.

### 6. Pouring Water
- **What it is:** The patient holds a cup of water (or beans) and pours it into another cup. 
- **What it tests:** **Task-Specific Tremor**. This is a highly complex kinetic movement requiring continuous pronation/supination (twisting of the wrist) and fine control. Tremors that severely disrupt daily living activities are very obvious here.

### 7. Spiral Moving
- **What it is:** The patient uses a pen to draw an Archimedes spiral on a piece of paper (or trace an existing one) without resting their hand on the table.
- **What it tests:** **Fine Motor Control**. This is the clinical "gold standard" for diagnosing Essential Tremor. Because the hand is constantly changing direction, the IMU data will perfectly capture the high-frequency rotational tremor, and the ink on the paper provides a visual severity score.
